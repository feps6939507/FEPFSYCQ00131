Abstract

Moddeling stock prices is a difficult task. The normal distribution is often used as an easy way to model how stock prices change over time. It assumes thatreturns move around an average value in a fairly even and predictable pattern. This makes it straightforward to estimate volatility and to work out how likely different price movements are. The approach has been important in the early development of financial models and risk-management tools. However, real markets do not always behave insuch a tidy way eg. the finacial crisis of 2008 and the DotCom bubble of 2000, where stock prices had a heavy decline. Stock returns can show sudden jumps and more extreme changes than the normal distribution allows for. Even so, it is still a useful starting point and a easy to understand simple benchmark for comparing more realistic models.

Introduction

One way to model a stock price and predict it's future movements is by using the normal distribution. It is a theoretical pattern that shows how values in a population are spread out. It's often called the bell curve because of its bell curved shape. When stock prices follow a normal pattern, most prices cluster around the mean, which is the highest point of the curve or the turning point of the graph. The normal distribution also uses standard deviation. A measure that shows how much values are spread out from the mean. A small standard deviation means the prices stay close to the average. Whilst a large standard deviation means the prices stay far away from the average. The area underneath the graph represents probability, so we can estime the likelyhood of a stock price to fall in a certain range. We can also calculate outliers by finding a lower fence. Doing two standard deviations left of the mean. To calculate the upper fence we do two standard deviations right of the mean.

Futhermore, to what extent is the normal distribution effective to model and predict future stock prices? Firstly, Using the normal distribution to model and predict future stock prices has several strengths, but it also comes with limitations. One advantage is its simplicity. The normal distribution is easy to work with, and many statistical tools are built around it, making calculations for expected returns, volatility (How fast prices change in the market), and risk more straightforward. This also makes it a useful teaching tool, helping new learners understand basic financial ideas. Another benefit is that it provides quick approximations, allowing analysts to estimate the likelihood of price movements without needing complex models. Because of its smooth, symmetric shape.

However, there are major drawbacks. Real stock market returns often show heavy tails, meaning extreme price jumps happen more often than a normal distribution predicts. This can lead to underestimating risk, especially during market crashes (COVID-19) or sudden events (TRUMP's TARRIFS) . The normal distribution also assumes symmetry, even though real returns often show slight skewness. In addition, it assumes a constant variance, while market volatility frequently changes over time. For these reasons, the normal distribution is useful as a baseline but not fully reliable for real-world prediction.
